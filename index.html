An Investigation into the Houston Astros Cheating Scandal¶

By Chandler Hayden, Fall 2019

Introduction¶

In November of 2019, the Houston Astros were accused of cheating during the 2017, 2018, and 2019 Major League Baseball seasons. Former Astros players admitted that the team cheated, and an email from a Houston executive was leaked which urged their scouts to spy on other teams. They are accused of using cameras and other technology during home games to steal the catcher's signs and then relay the information to their batters so that they would have an advantage by knowing what pitch was coming next. The Astros won the World Series in 2017 and again made it to the World Series in 2019. We will focus our investigation on the 2017 season when they won the World Series after not even making the playoffs the previous year.

Using statistics from the past three seasons and further historical data, we will investigate to see if their are any anomalies that may suggest that the Houston Astros had an unfair advantage working in their favor.

For more information: https://www.cbssports.com/mlb/news/astros-sign-stealing-scandal-what-to-know-about-cheating-allegations-and-mlbs-investigation/

After cleaning up that giant DataFrame, we can now see clearly how the Astros did in home games and away games. They play exactly half of their games at home and half away, or 81 games each. In 2019, they won 60 games at home, and only 47 on the road. If you look at the win percentages, at home they won 74% of games. In this table, there are four stats that I'm interested in. First is S/Str. S/Str is the number of strikes swung at without contact divided by the total number of strikes.
Next is I/Str. I/Str is balls put into play divided by total strikes. Third is Con which is the percentage of strikes that they made contact with. The last is S/SO which is the number of strikeouts swinging. I'll make a bar plot to help visualize S/Str.

Looking at this plot and table, we see that the Astros led the MLB in all four of these stats. First, they had the lowest S/Str. In baseball, batters are most likely to swing and miss when they are expecting a breaking ball and it's actually a fastball or vice versa. In other words, when they guess the next pitch wrong. If a batter knew what pitch was coming and didn't have to guess, their S/Str should be lower, as it is for the Astros.
Next, the Astros had the highest I/Str. Again, if a batter didn't have to guess which pitch is coming, it would be easier to put balls into play.
Similarly, the Astros led the MLB in Con.
Finally, the Astros had the least S/SO. This stat is correlated to the one looked at in 3.3.

5. Conclusion¶

After going through the data pipeline and analyzing the statistics, there is not much, if anything at all, to hint that the Astros were cheating. While the data analysis from 3.2, 3.3, and 3.4 make it appear that they may of had an advantage that resulted in them being the best team, there could be other reasons for this improvement other than cheating. For example, each year rosters change and they get rid of poor performing players and get new, hopefully better players. Players also sometimes just improve as a result of practice and experience.
It's also possible that there is some bias in the data analysis, because it is common that when you have an end result in mind (that the Astros cheated), you look for the data that confirms that result and ignore data that points against it. Overall, statistics can't be used to prove or disprove the allegations. It's possible that they cheated only in important game situations, meaning that their overall statistics wouldn't change much. MLB's on-going investigation will have to be more focused on the video evidence and other sources rather than any statistics.
